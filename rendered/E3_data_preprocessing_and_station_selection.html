notebook E.3 — Data Preprocessing and Station Selection¶

Thesis: Drought Modeling and Probabilistic Forecasting in the Chélif Basin (Algeria) Inputs: outputs of notebook E.2 (data audit and structuring) Outputs: SPI-ready precipitation panel with controlled interpolation and full provenance tracking

Purpose and scope¶

This notebook transforms the raw, grid-expanded precipitation dataset produced by notebook E.2 into a controlled, quality-assured precipitation panel suitable for direct input to the SPI Gamma distribution fitting procedure.

The operations performed here are, in order:

  1. Restrict the dataset to the selected study period (1990–2015)
  2. Replace physically impossible sentinel values (−999) with explicit NaN
  3. Evaluate station reliability through completeness and gap analysis
  4. Select stations under three conjunctive eligibility conditions (C1, C2, C3)
  5. Apply controlled linear interpolation to fill gaps ≤ 3 consecutive months
  6. Track every imputed value with an explicit provenance flag
  7. Validate the final dataset and quantify the interpolation burden

What this notebook does NOT do:

  • Compute SPI (that is the scope of notebook E.4)
  • Impute missing values blindly or beyond the 3-month ceiling
  • Remove stations without explicit, logged justification

Every transformation is deterministic, logged, and reproducible. All modified values are explicitly flagged, ensuring full transparency of preprocessing steps.

Section 2 — Sentinel Replacement, Period Restriction, and Grid Alignment¶

2.1 Sentinel value replacement¶

Inspection of the full grid reveals that the pluvio_chlef bulletin archive encoded missing observations as −999.0 rather than as NaN. This is a documented archival convention, but −999 is a physically impossible precipitation value and must be replaced with explicit NaN before any other operation.

Downstream consequence: Feeding −999 into the Gamma distribution fitter would seriously bias the lower tail of the fitted distribution, producing grossly erroneous SPI values for affected stations and months.

2.2 Period restriction and canonical grid alignment¶

The dataset is filtered to [1990-01-01, 2015-12-01] as established in notebook E.2. However, the full-grid CSV was constructed per-station using pd.date_range(first_obs, last_obs) — meaning stations whose last observed date falls before 2015-12-01 have no rows (not NaN rows) for the trailing months of the study period.

A simple date-range filter therefore produces fewer than 312 rows for those stations, which would cause the V2 structural validation to fail and, more importantly, would cause the completeness ratio to be computed against the wrong denominator.

Fix: after slicing, a canonical scaffold of 312 monthly dates is constructed for every station and the observations are left-joined onto it. Months absent from the source archive appear as NaN rows — the correct and fully honest representation. This is a structural alignment step, not imputation.

Section 3 — Station Completeness Analysis¶

For each of the 32 stations, we compute the pre-fill completeness ratio:

$$c_{\text{before}}(i) = \frac{n_{\text{obs}}(i)}{N} \qquad N = 312$$

where $n_{\text{obs}}(i)$ is the count of non-NaN months. This ratio determines eligibility under Condition C1.

Note that the sentinel replacement in Section 2.1 may have reduced completeness for some stations relative to what notebook E.2 reported — those reductions are legitimate corrections, not errors.

Section 4 — Station Selection¶

Three conjunctive eligibility conditions are applied sequentially. A station must satisfy all three to enter the final analytical panel.

Condition C1 — Minimum pre-fill completeness¶

$$c_{\text{before}}(i) \geq 0.90$$

Why 90 %? The 90 % threshold is an operational modeling choice selected to balance data quality and sample size, and is consistent with common practices in hydrological time-series preprocessing. It ensures that the imputed fraction of any retained station's series remains at most 10 % — the level beyond which Gamma distribution parameter estimates may become less stable, since imputed values contribute to the empirical distribution used for fitting. A lower threshold would admit stations where imputed values begin to dominate the tails of the empirical distribution, which can introduce bias into SPI extremes. A higher threshold would unnecessarily reduce spatial coverage of the panel.

This is a deliberate trade-off: spatial density is sacrificed in exchange for reducing dependence on imputed values in the statistical fitting step.

Condition C2 — Maximum contiguous gap¶

$$g_{\max}(i) \leq 3 \text{ months}$$

The 3-month ceiling is likewise an operational modeling choice that is consistent with common practice in hydrological preprocessing. A gap of 1–3 months in a monthly precipitation record can reasonably be bridged by linear interpolation, under the assumption that the true value varies smoothly between the adjacent observed months. Gaps of 4 or more consecutive months span entire seasons, making this smooth-variation assumption increasingly difficult to justify — the interpolated values may misrepresent the seasonal cycle and could introduce artificial structure into the SPI series.

Stations with gaps > 3 months are therefore excluded from the analytical panel. This decision was therefore incorporated into the station selection stage before any interpolation is applied, ensuring that the interpolation step operates only on gaps that satisfy the continuity assumption.

Condition C3 — Manual exclusion¶

Station M_011509__11509 satisfies C1 and C2 but is manually excluded under C3. Manual exclusions are reserved for stations where a quantitative anomaly cannot be resolved by completeness or gap criteria alone — typically where the record structure suggests a non-climatic data problem that would silently contaminate the index construction.

# ── 4.1  Compute max contiguous gap per station ───────────────────────────────
def max_contiguous_gap(series: pd.Series) -> int:
    """Return the length of the longest run of NaN values."""
    max_gap = cur = 0
    for v in series:
        if pd.isna(v): cur += 1; max_gap = max(max_gap, cur)
        else: cur = 0
    return max_gap

Section 6 — Gap Analysis¶

A gap is a contiguous sequence of one or more missing months within a station's record. The gap length determines whether linear interpolation is applicable.

For each station, we enumerate all gaps, record their start date and length, and classify them as:

  • Fillable (length ≤ 3 months): eligible for linear interpolation
  • Unfillable (length > 3 months): must remain as NaN (no station in the retained panel has such gaps — this was enforced by Condition C2)

This section produces the full gap inventory that drives the interpolation logic in Section 8.

Section 7 — Definition of the Interpolation Rule¶

Rule¶

Linear interpolation is applied if and only if the contiguous gap length satisfies:

$$g \leq 3 \text{ months}$$

Formally, for a missing observation at month $t$ within a gap of length $g$ bounded by observed values $P_{t-1}$ (last observed before the gap) and $P_{t+g}$ (first observed after the gap):

$$\hat{P}_{t+k} = P_{t-1} + (k+1) \cdot \frac{P_{t+g} - P_{t-1}}{g+1} \qquad k = 0, 1, \ldots, g-1$$

Why linear interpolation?¶

Monthly precipitation does not exhibit strong nonlinear short-term structure (unlike temperature, which may have quadratic or exponential components). Linear interpolation is the simplest defensible choice under the assumption that the true value varies smoothly between the two adjacent observations. It introduces no distributional assumptions beyond local continuity, is fully deterministic, and is a standard approach in the SPI literature (Guttman, 1999; McKee et al., 1993).

Why the 3-month ceiling?¶

The 3-month ceiling is an operational modeling choice that was incorporated at the station-selection stage (Condition C2), not enforced solely by the interpolation procedure itself. By excluding stations with gaps > 3 months before any interpolation is applied, we ensure that the interpolation step operates exclusively on gaps that satisfy the smooth-variation assumption implicit in linear estimation. The limit parameter of pandas.Series.interpolate is used as a secondary programmatic safeguard — an additional layer of protection against accidental application to longer gaps — but the primary guarantee is structural: no retained station can have a gap exceeding 3 months, because such stations were excluded under C2.

Substantive justification for the 3-month limit:

  • Gap of 1 month: the interpolated value lies between two adjacent monthly totals, both observed. The estimate is well-constrained and the interpolation error is bounded by the month-to-month variability.
  • Gap of 2 months: spans at most ~60 days; linear interpolation introduces at most one intermediate seasonal step, which remains reasonable for accumulation-based indices.
  • Gap of 3 months: the maximum gap where the interpolation can be argued to remain within a single seasonal quadrant (e.g., entirely within the wet season or entirely within the dry season). Beyond 3 months, the linear path crosses season boundaries and the imputed values may be systematically biased relative to the true seasonal climatology.

Why not impute larger gaps?¶

Gaps of 4 or more months span entire seasons. Applying linear interpolation across such gaps would:

  1. Risk misrepresenting the seasonal precipitation distribution and thereby potentially biasing the per-month Gamma fits on which SPI depends.
  2. Potentially introduce artificial smoothness at the gap boundaries, which could inflate the apparent autocorrelation of the series.
  3. Inflate the effective number of observations for calendar months where the record is structurally absent, undermining the validity of the fitted distribution for those months.

For these reasons, the interpolation burden was deliberately minimised — applied only where the structural and seasonal continuity assumptions are most defensible.

Statistical integrity of the retained panel¶

The aggregate interpolated fraction across the panel is 1.08 % of all station-month cells. At this level, Gamma distribution parameters are dominated by directly observed values, and the contribution of imputed values to parameter estimation is negligible. Every imputed value is flagged in the was_interpolated column, enabling downstream sensitivity analyses to assess the impact of interpolation on SPI results.

Section 8 — Apply Controlled Linear Interpolation¶

The interpolation is applied per station using pandas.Series.interpolate with method='linear' and limit=INTERP_MAX_GAP. As established in Section 7, the primary guarantee that no gap exceeding 3 months is filled comes from the station-selection stage (Condition C2): all retained stations have maximum contiguous gaps ≤ 3 months by construction. The limit parameter serves as an additional programmatic safeguard to make this constraint explicit in the interpolation call and to protect against any edge case that might arise from data manipulation between sections.

A boolean column was_interpolated is created to flag every value that was imputed by this step. This column is preserved in all downstream outputs, ensuring that any analysis can identify and optionally downweight imputed observations.

Column conventions:

  • precip_mm — raw observed precipitation, preserved unchanged (NaN where missing in source archives)
  • precip_filled — the series used for SPI computation; identical to precip_mm for observed months, linearly interpolated for gap months
  • was_interpolated — True only for months where precip_mm is NaN and precip_filled was estimated by interpolation

All transformations are explicitly tracked, ensuring full reproducibility and transparency of the preprocessing steps.

Section 10 — Interpolation Impact on Rolling Accumulation Windows¶

SPI at scale $k$ is computed from the $k$-month rolling sum:

$$X_t^{(k)} = \sum_{i=0}^{k-1} P_{t-i}$$

If one or more of the $k$ terms in this sum is an interpolated value, the accuracy of $X_t^{(k)}$ is reduced. In the worst case (a 3-month gap entirely within a 3-month window), SPI-3 would be computed entirely from interpolated values at that time step — the extreme case to be flagged and monitored.

For each time step and each station, we compute:

  • n_interp_in_window_3: count of interpolated months in the SPI-3 window
  • n_interp_in_window_6: count of interpolated months in the SPI-6 window
  • n_interp_in_window_12: count of interpolated months in the SPI-12 window

This table allows downstream analysis to flag SPI values where the rolling sum contains a high imputed fraction.

# ── 10.1  Compute rolling interpolation counts ────────────────────────────────
frames = []
for sid, grp in precip_interp.sort_values([STATION_COL, DATE_COL]).groupby(STATION_COL):
    g = grp.copy().reset_index(drop=True)
    interp_int = g["was_interpolated"].astype(int)
    g["n_interp_in_window_3"]  = interp_int.rolling(3,  min_periods=1).sum().astype(int)
    g["n_interp_in_window_6"]  = interp_int.rolling(6,  min_periods=1).sum().astype(int)
    g["n_interp_in_window_12"] = interp_int.rolling(12, min_periods=1).sum().astype(int)
    frames.append(g)

Section 14 — Interpretation and Chapter Summary¶

What was retained and why¶

Of the 32 candidate stations in the master pool, 9 stations are retained in the final analytical panel after applying three conjunctive eligibility conditions. The three conditions and their outcomes:

  • C1 (completeness ≥ 90 %): computed against the full 312-month denominator after scaffold expansion. Stations whose archive ends before December 2015 receive their absent trailing months as explicit NaN rows under the scaffold alignment, which correctly reduces their completeness ratio below the 90 % threshold. This is not a modeling assumption but an accurate representation of data availability. 16 stations fail C1.
  • C2 (max contiguous gap ≤ 3 months): OLD_11803 passes C1 (c = 0.949) but contains a 5-month contiguous gap in 2015. Under the 3-month operational ceiling, this gap cannot be filled by linear interpolation, disqualifying the station.
  • C3 (structural discontinuity): M_011509__11509 has complete coverage (c = 1.000) but is manually excluded due to a step discontinuity of non-climatic origin that would distort the regional SPI series and the spatial coherence analysis. The exclusion is fully documented in Section 4 and logged in station_selection_table.csv.

How much interpolation was applied¶

36 months of a total 2,808 station-month cells (1.08 %) were estimated by linear interpolation. This fraction is well within the range where its influence on Gamma distribution parameter estimation is negligible. The imputation is concentrated in OLD_11702 (10 months) and OLD_11718 (9 months), both of which carry isolated single-month gaps distributed across the 26-year record. No station has an SPI-12 accumulation window composed entirely of imputed values.

Why the scaffold expansion is methodologically necessary¶

The full grid produced by notebook E.2 extends each station only to its last observed date. Stations that stopped reporting before 2015-12-01 therefore had no rows — not NaN rows — for the trailing months of the study window. A simple date-range filter would have silently produced a structurally inconsistent panel (some stations with < 312 rows) and computed completeness ratios against an incorrect denominator. The scaffold expansion makes all absences explicit NaN observations before any eligibility computation is performed. This is a structural alignment step, not imputation.

Why the preprocessing assumptions are defensible¶

  1. Physical plausibility: all interpolated values are positive and lie between positive observed values on either side of the gap.
  2. Statistical minimality: the 1.08 % imputed fraction is negligible; Gamma distribution parameters are driven overwhelmingly by the 98.92 % of directly observed months.
  3. Full provenance tracking: the was_interpolated column flags every imputed value in the output file, enabling downstream sensitivity analyses.
  4. Seasonal conservatism: the 3-month ceiling tends to keep interpolation within a single seasonal quadrant, reducing (though not eliminating) the risk of systematic bias in per-month empirical distributions.
  5. Threshold transparency: the 90 % and 3-month thresholds are stated as operational modeling choices, not universal constants, and their effect on network size is explicitly quantified in Section 4.
